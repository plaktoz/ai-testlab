Aquatic Ecosystems: The Pond

Lesson 06 · ~30 minutes

A spoonful of healthy pond water contains thousands of living organisms you cannot see.

Cyanobacteria, diatoms, rotifers, copepods, flagellates, Daphnia — the invisible machinery of the aquatic food web. Every fish in that pond depends on this broth. Every amphibian, dragonfly, and kingfisher depends on it. Remove those invisible millions and the visible ecosystem collapses.

Ponds look simple: water, some plants, a few fish. They're not. They're layered, stratified, chemically complex systems where oxygen, light, temperature, and nutrients all vary with depth — and all interact with the biology at every level.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: describe the light and temperature zones of a pond, explain why dissolved oxygen varies with depth, define eutrophication and trace its mechanism from fertiliser to dead zone, and diagnose a pond's health from a set of clues.

Water Is Not Just Water

Terrestrial ecosystems have soil as their medium; aquatic ecosystems have water. But water is not a neutral background — it's an active component that determines everything about what can live where.

Key physical properties that structure aquatic life:

  • Light attenuation: Water absorbs and scatters light. Depth at which enough light exists for photosynthesis = the photic zone. Below it = the aphotic zone (darkness). In a clear lake, the photic zone extends ~30–100m. In murky water (turbid, algae-rich), it may be less than 1m.
  • Temperature stratification: Warm water floats on cold. In summer, pond water stratifies into a warm upper layer (epilimnion) and cold lower layer (hypolimnion), separated by a rapidly transitioning zone (thermocline). This stratification limits mixing and has huge consequences for oxygen and nutrients.
  • Dissolved oxygen (DO): Oxygen dissolves in water from the atmosphere and from photosynthesis. Cold water holds more DO than warm water. The bottom sediments often have very low DO (aerobic decomposers use it up; no photosynthesis adds it). Fish need DO to breathe.

Q: In summer, a deep lake's surface water is 24°C. Its bottom water is 6°C. An algae bloom occurs near the surface, thickening the water with green particles. What happens to the photic zone?

Dense algae (and suspended particles generally) scatter and absorb light, reducing transparency. The photic zone shrinks from the bottom upward — light no longer reaches deep water. Aquatic plants below the bloom receive no light and die. This is one of the mechanisms behind eutrophication: the bloom shades everything below it. The paradox: the very organisms that need light eventually create conditions where light can't penetrate deeply.

The Producers: Phytoplankton and Macrophytes

Two types of producers in a pond:

Phytoplankton

Microscopic, single-celled photosynthesisers floating in the water column: diatoms, green algae, cyanobacteria. They're invisible to the naked eye but collectively outproduce all terrestrial forests in the ocean. In a healthy pond, they're the base of the food web — grazed by zooplankton (Daphnia, copepods), which are eaten by small fish, which are eaten by larger fish.

Think of phytoplankton as an invisible floating forest. The forest is there; you just need a microscope to see the trees.

Macrophytes

The visible plants: reeds (Phragmites), water lilies (Nuphar), pondweed (Potamogeton). They anchor the edges, provide habitat for invertebrates and fish fry, and absorb nutrients directly from sediment. They're less important as food (most are too tough to eat) than as habitat structure.

The primary productivity split: In open water, phytoplankton dominate production. Near shallow edges and banks, macrophytes and attached algae (periphyton) dominate. The ratio shifts with nutrient levels — nutrient-rich ponds tend to be dominated by phytoplankton blooms; nutrient-poor, clear ponds have more macrophytes. This matters because macrophyte-dominated ponds are more stable and support more biodiversity.

Q: Two ponds have equal surface area. Pond A is nutrient-poor and clear, dominated by macrophytes. Pond B is nutrient-rich and murky, dominated by algal phytoplankton. Which supports more fish species?

Macrophyte-dominated systems support more species because: (1) physical structure diversity (stems, leaves, root zones) creates microhabitats for invertebrates and fish fry; (2) the system is more stable — algal blooms are unpredictable and their crashes cause oxygen crashes that kill fish; (3) greater food web complexity. Pond B may produce more algal biomass, but boom-bust cycles reduce biodiversity. Ecologists call these "alternative stable states" — a pond can flip from macrophyte-dominated to algae-dominated and vice versa.

Oxygen in Aquatic Systems

Fish and aquatic invertebrates breathe dissolved oxygen (DO). Understanding where DO comes from and where it goes is essential for diagnosing any aquatic problem.

Sources of DO:

  • Atmospheric diffusion at the surface (slow, weather-dependent)
  • Photosynthesis by phytoplankton and macrophytes (during daylight only)
  • Water movement/aeration (turbulence oxygenates)

Sinks for DO:

  • Respiration of all aerobic organisms (constant)
  • Decomposition of organic matter in sediments (especially in warm, nutrient-rich ponds)
  • Stratification: warm surface layers become isolated from deeper water, which deplete their DO
The daily oxygen cycle: In a pond with abundant phytoplankton, DO actually changes throughout the day. Midday: photosynthesis high → DO rises. Midnight: no photosynthesis, respiration continues → DO falls. Fish die most often just before dawn — lowest DO of the day. Aquarium and pond keepers who find dead fish in the morning often wrongly blame a toxin; it's usually oxygen depletion overnight.

Q: A pond has dense algae covering the surface. On a sunny day, fish are gasping at the surface. On a cloudy day, more fish die. Explain both observations using dissolved oxygen.

On sunny days: photosynthesis adds oxygen, but algae at depth respire. Fish gasp at the surface because the only well-oxygenated water is at the very top (photosynthesis + atmospheric diffusion). On cloudy days: no photosynthesis — all the algae shift to pure respiration, consuming DO rapidly. The whole water column goes hypoxic. Fish die. Dense algae blooms create this paradox: the organisms producing oxygen also consume it, and their sheer abundance means they can crash the system when light fails.

Eutrophication: When Nutrients Kill a Lake

Eutrophication means "over-enrichment". It's what happens when a lake receives too much nitrogen and phosphorus — usually from agricultural fertiliser runoff, sewage, or detergent. The mechanism:

Fertiliser runoff (NO₃⁻, PO₄³⁻) → Algal bloom (explosive phytoplankton growth) → Shades out underwater plants → Plants die → decomposers consume DO → Hypoxia (very low O₂) → Fish and invertebrates die: dead zone

There's also a toxic dimension: some cyanobacterial blooms produce toxins (microcystin) that are lethal to mammals. Dogs die drinking from affected lakes. Shellfish concentrate the toxins. The nutrient that was supposed to grow crops ends up killing an entire lake ecosystem.

Real example: Lake Erie (North America) had catastrophic algal blooms from the 1960s–70s due to phosphorus runoff. So severe that large areas became anoxic dead zones. It was declared "dead" in popular press. International phosphorus regulations in the 1970s–80s reversed it — partial recovery by the 1990s. Now deteriorating again due to agricultural intensity increases.

Q: A pond near farms starts showing green scum on the surface in summer. Fish are found dead in the mornings. Which investigation would best confirm eutrophication as the cause?

Option B directly tests the two diagnostic markers: nutrient levels (confirming the cause — excess nitrogen/phosphorus driving the bloom) and oxygen levels (confirming the mechanism — hypoxia at depth/overnight killing the fish). Option C detects the effect but not the cause. Option D is relevant (warm water holds less O₂) but secondary to the eutrophication diagnosis. In environmental science, a good diagnosis pairs the input cause (elevated nutrients) with the mechanism (oxygen depletion) — not just the symptom (dead fish).

The Pond as a System

Return to Lesson 01's framework: inputs, outputs, cycling, feedback.

  • Inputs: sunlight, rain, nutrients from inflow streams, atmospheric O₂/CO₂
  • Outputs: evaporation, outflow streams, CO₂ and O₂ gas exchange, sediment
  • Internal cycling: nitrogen cycle (fixation in sediments, nitrification, plant assimilation), carbon cycle (photosynthesis → respiration → decomposition)
  • Feedback: more algae → less DO → fewer fish → less grazing on zooplankton → more zooplankton → more phytoplankton grazing → less algae (a stabilising loop — if not overwhelmed by nutrient input)

Quick Summary

  • Ponds are stratified by temperature and light — the photic zone is where photosynthesis occurs
  • Primary producers: phytoplankton (microscopic, open water) and macrophytes (visible plants, edges)
  • Dissolved oxygen comes from photosynthesis and atmosphere; consumed by respiration and decomposition
  • Eutrophication: excess nutrients → algal bloom → oxygen crash → dead zone
  • A healthy pond is macrophyte-dominated, clear, and stable; a eutrophic pond is algae-dominated, murky, and prone to crashes
