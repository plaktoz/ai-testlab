Food Webs: The Messy Reality

Lesson 03 · ~30 minutes

A single grey wolf in Yellowstone can affect the shape of rivers.

Not because wolves dig. Not because wolves drink a lot. Because wolves hunt elk. Elk — knowing wolves are around — avoid open riverbanks where escape is difficult. So the vegetation on those banks grows back. Tree roots hold the banks together. The rivers narrow and deepen. Beaver return (more willow to eat). Beaver dams create ponds. New wetland habitats appear.

This is a trophic cascade. One wolf changed the physical landscape through a chain of predator–prey interactions. Food chains don't capture this. Only webs do.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: explain why food chains are a simplification, describe what redundancy is and why it matters for stability, define keystone species and give a real example, and trace the consequences of removing a single species from a food web.

The Problem with Food Chains

Textbook: "Grass → Rabbit → Fox." Neat. Clean. Wrong.

What does a real fox eat? Rabbits, yes — but also mice, voles, beetles, berries, eggs, earthworms, dead birds, and the occasional sandwich stolen from a picnic. What eats the rabbit? Foxes, yes — but also stoats, weasels, red kites, buzzards, owls, and the occasional large pike if the rabbit falls in a river.

A food chain is a cross-section through a food web. It's a useful lie — it makes the energy flow legible. But it strips out everything that matters when the system is under stress.

Q: A food chain shows Grass → Rabbit → Fox → Eagle. The rabbit population crashes due to a disease. What does a food chain predict happens next?

The food chain predicts clean upward collapse — fewer rabbits → fewer foxes → fewer eagles. In reality (the food web), foxes switch to mice and voles, eagles switch to pheasants and carrion, so the actual impact is buffered. The food chain is a worst-case prediction. Real webs are more resilient — but the chain still tells you the direction of stress.

Redundancy: The Hidden Safety Net

In a food web, multiple species often do the same job. Several herbivores eat the same plant. Several predators eat the same prey. This is redundancy — and it's the main reason ecosystems survive shocks.

Imagine an electrical circuit. Single wire connecting A to B: cut the wire, the circuit dies. Ten parallel wires connecting A to B: cut one, current re-routes. Ecosystems with high species diversity have many parallel wires. Ecosystems with low species diversity are single-wire circuits — one failure can cascade.

Redundancy rule: The more species fill each functional role (herbivore, seed disperser, pollinator, decomposer), the more shocks the system can absorb before collapsing. Biodiversity is not decoration. It's the circuit backup.

Q: Ecosystem A has 3 species of bee pollinating its flowers. Ecosystem B has 12 species of bee doing the same job. A new pesticide kills all but 2 bee species in each ecosystem. Which ecosystem is more likely to maintain normal plant reproduction?

Ecosystem B keeps 10 species (83% of its bee community). Ecosystem A keeps 2 (67%). But the numbers alone aren't the point — B still has abundant redundancy. Even with losses, multiple species still cover the pollination role. Ecosystem A is functionally down to minimum viable coverage. This is why conservationists don't just count species — they count functional redundancy.

Keystone Species: When One Species Holds the Arch

In Roman arch construction, the keystone is the wedge-shaped stone at the top of the arch. Remove it and the whole arch falls — despite the keystone being one of the smaller pieces. In ecology, a keystone species has a disproportionately large effect on its ecosystem relative to its own abundance.

Three classic examples:

1. Sea Otters (North Pacific)

Sea otters eat sea urchins. Sea urchins eat kelp. Without otters, urchins multiply and devour kelp forests. Kelp forests shelter fish, seabirds, seals, and invertebrates — the whole Pacific nearshore ecosystem depends on otters keeping urchins in check. When fur hunters nearly wiped out otters in the 1800s, vast kelp forests collapsed into "urchin barrens" — underwater deserts.

2. Wolves (Yellowstone)

Already told you. Wolves → elk behaviour → riverbank vegetation → river shape → beaver → wetlands. Their effect on the physical landscape exceeds what their biomass would predict.

3. African Elephants

Elephants knock down trees and dig waterholes. The waterholes serve hundreds of other species during dry season. The knocked-down trees create grasslands that support grazers. Without elephants, many African savannas would close into dense woodland, losing the grassland species entirely.

Q: Sea urchins are very common in a Pacific bay. Sea otters are rare. Scientists remove all otters from the bay. What do they observe 2 years later?

This is a real experiment that happened naturally via fur hunting in the 1800s and was studied in the 1960s by ecologist Robert Paine. Otter removal → urchin explosion → kelp collapse → loss of all the species that depend on kelp habitat. The otters were rare, but they were the keystone. Abundance doesn't predict ecological importance — function does.

Trophic Cascades

A trophic cascade is when a change at one level of the food web ripples up or down and affects distant levels — often in unexpected ways.

Top-down cascade: Predator changes → prey changes → plant changes. Wolves removed → elk multiply → vegetation grazed down → erosion increases → streams destabilize. The effect "cascades down" from the top.

Bottom-up cascade: Producer changes → herbivore changes → predator changes. Fertiliser runoff → algae bloom → phytoplankton crash (shaded out) → zooplankton crash (no food) → small fish crash → large fish crash. The effect "cascades up" from the bottom.

Rule of thumb: Top-down cascades are driven by predation — changes in who eats whom. Bottom-up cascades are driven by productivity — changes in how much energy enters the base. Real ecosystems have both happening simultaneously, which is why predicting the full outcome of any single change is genuinely hard.

Q: A commercial fishery removes 80% of the cod population from the North Atlantic. Cod eat herring. Herring eat zooplankton. Zooplankton eat phytoplankton. What type of cascade results, and what happens to phytoplankton?

This is a top-down cascade. Fewer cod means herring face less predation and multiply. More herring eat more zooplankton. Fewer zooplankton means less grazing pressure on phytoplankton — so phytoplankton increases. This cascade has been documented in real North Atlantic data following decades of overfishing. Each "wrong" answer misses one link in the chain. Trace it step by step: cod ↓ → herring ↑ → zooplankton ↓ → phytoplankton ↑.

Species Removal: A Worked Example

Consider this simplified African savanna web:

Savanna food web (simplified)

Producers: Grass, Acacia, Shrubs
Herbivores: Zebra, Wildebeest, Elephant, Impala
Carnivores: Lion, Cheetah, Wild Dog
Scavengers/Decomposers: Vulture, Hyena, Bacteria/Fungi

Key connections: Lion eats zebra, wildebeest, impala · Cheetah eats impala, gazelle · Wild dog eats wildebeest, impala · Elephant knocks trees → creates grassland · Hyena scavenges all kills · Vultures clean carcasses quickly

Question: Suppose wild dogs go extinct in this ecosystem. Trace the effects.

Direct effect: Impala and wildebeest face less predation. Their populations grow.

Indirect effect on plants: More wildebeest → more grass consumed → grass patches shrink. Open areas increase, which changes which plant species dominate.

Indirect effect on competitors: More prey available for lions and cheetahs → their populations may grow slightly → more intense competition within carnivore guild.

Unexpected effect: Wild dogs are highly efficient hunters — they often leave carcasses quickly, and vultures depend on them. Fewer wild dog kills → fewer easy scavenging opportunities for vultures → vulture numbers may decline.

Q: Using the savanna example above, if elephants are removed by poaching, what is the most likely long-term effect on grassland-dwelling species like zebra?

Elephants are ecosystem engineers that maintain open savanna by pushing over trees. Without elephants, bush encroachment converts grassland to woodland. Zebra are grazers — they need open grass, not closed woodland. Less grassland = fewer zebra, despite there being no direct predator-prey link between elephants and zebra. This is an indirect effect operating through habitat modification — classic food web complexity that a simple chain would miss entirely.

Quick Summary

  • Food chains are simplified cross-sections of food webs — useful but incomplete
  • Redundancy: multiple species performing the same function gives systems resilience to shock
  • Keystone species: disproportionate effect relative to their abundance (otters, wolves, elephants)
  • Trophic cascades: top-down (predator → prey → plants) or bottom-up (plants → prey → predator)
  • Removing any species causes indirect effects — some predictable, some surprising
  • Real ecology is about connections, not individual species
