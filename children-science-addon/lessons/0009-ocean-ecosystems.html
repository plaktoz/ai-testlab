Ocean Ecosystems

Lesson 09 · ~30 minutes

In 1977, a submersible crew descended to 2,500 metres below the Pacific Ocean and found something that rewrote biology.

At that depth, there is no sunlight. Temperatures hover near freezing. The pressure is 250 times atmospheric. By every model of life that existed at the time, nothing should be there.

But there were thriving ecosystems. Giant tube worms (1.5 metres tall). Clams the size of dinner plates. Shrimp, crabs, fish. Entire communities dense with life — around cracks in the ocean floor venting superheated water laced with hydrogen sulphide.

They were hydrothermal vent communities. And they don't run on sunlight at all. They run on chemistry. The discovery proved that life does not require the sun — only energy. That single finding expanded the definition of a habitable environment to include the deep ocean, ice-covered moons like Europa, and anywhere in the universe where chemical energy gradients exist.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: describe the zones of the ocean and what organisms live in each, explain chemosynthesis and how it differs from photosynthesis, describe a whale fall as a temporary ecosystem, and compare ocean to terrestrial ecosystems using the framework from Lessons 01–06.

The Ocean in Numbers

First principles start with scale:

  • 71% of Earth's surface is ocean
  • 97% of Earth's water is in the ocean
  • Average ocean depth: 3,688 metres
  • Deepest point: Mariana Trench, 11,034 metres (deeper than Everest is tall)
  • Volume: 1.335 billion km³ — 99% of Earth's inhabitable space by volume is ocean
  • Temperature range: −2°C (polar ice water) to 400°C+ (hydrothermal vents)

The ocean is not one ecosystem. It is a stack of very different ecosystems, layered by depth.

Zones of the Ocean

Ocean Depth Zones

Zone Depth Light Key organisms
Sunlit (Epipelagic) 0–200m Full sunlight Phytoplankton, zooplankton, most fish, sharks, dolphins, seabirds (diving)
Twilight (Mesopelagic) 200–1,000m Faint, no photosynthesis Lanternfish, hatchetfish, squid — many migrate vertically each night
Midnight (Bathypelagic) 1,000–4,000m No light Anglerfish, deep-sea jellyfish, bioluminescent organisms
Abyss (Abyssopelagic) 4,000–6,000m None Sea cucumbers, brittle stars, rare fish — slow-moving, low-energy lifestyles
Hadal (Trenches) >6,000m None Hadal amphipods, snailfish — ultra-high pressure specialists
Hydrothermal Vents Any deep depth None Tube worms, vent clams, chemosynthetic bacteria — energy from chemistry

The Sunlit Zone: Phytoplankton as an Invisible Forest

The epipelagic zone (0–200m) contains all photosynthesis-based ocean primary production. Phytoplankton are the producers: microscopic single-celled organisms (diatoms, cyanobacteria, dinoflagellates, coccolithophores) that use sunlight, CO₂, and dissolved nutrients to produce organic carbon.

Phytoplankton collectively generate approximately 50% of the oxygen in Earth's atmosphere. More than all terrestrial forests combined. The rainforests you've heard called "the lungs of the Earth" contribute about 20%; the actual lungs are invisible, floating in the top 200 metres of the ocean.

Q: Iron is a limiting nutrient for phytoplankton in much of the open ocean — there's plenty of nitrogen and phosphorus but iron is scarce. Scientists fertilise a patch of ocean with iron powder. What happens and why does this matter for atmospheric CO₂?

This is a real experiment — the "iron hypothesis" proposed by John Martin in 1990. Add iron → phytoplankton bloom → rapid CO₂ fixation via photosynthesis. When the bloom sinks (the "biological pump"), carbon is exported to the deep ocean and potentially sequestered for centuries. Multiple ocean iron fertilisation experiments (IRONEX, SOIREE, LOHAFEX) confirmed the bloom effect. The long-term carbon sequestration is less certain — much of the carbon is re-mineralised before reaching depth. It's actively studied as a potential climate intervention, with unresolved ecological risks.

Chemosynthesis: Life Without the Sun

At hydrothermal vents, seawater seeps through cracks in the ocean floor, is heated to 300–400°C by magma beneath, picks up minerals including hydrogen sulphide (H₂S), and gushes back out as superheated water. The chemical energy in H₂S is the energy source for an entirely different kind of primary production.

Chemosynthetic bacteria do not use sunlight. They use chemical reactions:

Photosynthesis vs Chemosynthesis

Photosynthesis:
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Chemosynthesis (sulphur-based, typical of vent bacteria):
CO₂ + 4H₂S + O₂ → CH₂O + 4S + 3H₂O + chemical energy

Both produce organic carbon (CH₂O or C₆H₁₂O₆ — same thing scaled) from CO₂. The difference: the energy source is chemical oxidation, not photons.

The tube worm (Riftia pachyptila) takes this a step further: it has no mouth, no gut, no digestive system. Its body contains an organ called the trophosome that is packed with billions of chemosynthetic bacteria. The worm supplies the bacteria with H₂S and CO₂; the bacteria supply the worm with organic carbon. The entire animal is a mobile bacteria farm — the ultimate mutualistic symbiosis.

Why this matters beyond marine biology: The discovery of chemosynthesis-based ecosystems separated "life" from "sunlight" for the first time. This makes icy moons like Europa (Jupiter) and Enceladus (Saturn) plausible candidates for life — they have water, rocky seafloors, and likely hydrothermal activity. The absence of sunlight is no longer disqualifying.

Q: A hydrothermal vent goes inactive — the superheated water stops flowing. The H₂S supply ends. What happens to the vent community?

Vent communities depend entirely on chemosynthesis as their primary production. No H₂S → no chemosynthetic bacteria → no primary production → entire food web collapses. Tube worms cannot photosynthesize (no sunlight at 2,500m) and cannot swim away — they're sessile (attached to the rock). Vent communities do collapse when vents go inactive; populations persist at the ocean scale because new vents open elsewhere and larval stages (from planktonic phases early in life) colonise them. The vent community is the most energy-precarious ecosystem on Earth — entirely dependent on one point-source chemical input.

Whale Falls: A Temporary Ecosystem

When a whale dies in the open ocean (average whale: 40,000 kg of biomass), it sinks. What happens next is one of the most extraordinary examples of ecological succession in nature.

A whale fall creates a temporary, multi-stage ecosystem that can last 50–100 years at one location on the ocean floor:

Whale Fall Succession — Four Stages

Stage 1 — Mobile scavengers (months):
Sharks, sleeper sharks, hagfish, grenadier fish — attracted from kilometres away. Remove 90% of soft tissue within 4–6 months. Up to 40,000 scavenger visits recorded at a single carcass.

Stage 2 — Enrichment opportunists (months to years):
Polychaete worms, crustaceans, and mobile invertebrates colonise exposed bones and remaining tissue. Bacterial mats form. Density of organisms is 100× the surrounding seafloor.

Stage 3 — Sulphophilic stage (decades):
As bacteria decompose whale lipids deep in the bone, H₂S is produced — the same as at hydrothermal vents. Chemosynthetic bacteria colonise. Vent-specialist organisms appear: Osedax worms (bone-eating snot-flower worms — actual name) drill into bone with acid-secreting roots. Mussels, clams, and crabs form communities identical in structure to hydrothermal vent communities.

Stage 4 — Reef stage (decades):
The chemosynthetic community exhausts the whale lipids. The bare skeleton becomes substrate for suspension feeders — sponges, sea anemones, crinoids. The skeleton is now colonised reef, supporting hundreds of species for decades after the last molecule of whale fat is gone.

Whale falls are so important that some deep-sea species appear to depend on them as "stepping stones" across the ocean floor — their larvae drift between whale falls, allowing vent-associated species to colonise new vents. A whale fall is not a death; it's a new ecosystem at the moment of landing.

Q: A deep-sea researcher finds organisms normally associated with hydrothermal vents living on a whale fall 1,500 km from the nearest known vent. How is this possible?

This is the "stepping stone" hypothesis, proposed by Craig Smith (University of Hawaii). The biochemistry of whale bone decomposition produces hydrogen sulphide — same energy substrate as hydrothermal vents. Vent-specialist organisms colonise the whale fall via planktonic larvae drifting in ocean currents. The whale fall sustains them for decades while they reproduce and their offspring drift on to the next whale fall or vent. Without whale falls as intermediate habitats, vent communities might not be able to colonise new vents across large ocean distances. Sperm whales, being deepest-diving, provide the most useful falls for this function.

Ocean vs Terrestrial: A Comparison

Using the framework from all previous lessons, how does the ocean differ from a forest?

Ocean vs Temperate Forest — Systems Comparison

Feature Temperate Forest Open Ocean
Primary producers Trees, shrubs, grasses Phytoplankton (microscopic)
Turnover rate Slow — trees live decades to centuries Fast — phytoplankton live 1–7 days
Primary productivity (g C/m²/yr) ~800 (temperate) / ~2,200 (tropical) ~125 (open ocean) / ~2,500 (coral reefs)
Decomposition location Soil (shallow) Deep ocean floor (via marine snow)
Nitrogen cycle Soil-based; roots assimilate Dissolved in water; phytoplankton assimilate
Energy source options Sunlight only Sunlight (shallow) + chemistry (deep vents)
Marine snow: Dead phytoplankton, zooplankton faeces, and aggregated organic particles sink continuously from the surface to the deep ocean floor. This "marine snow" is the main food supply for most of the deep ocean ecosystem. Everything below the photic zone ultimately depends on the productivity at the surface — except vent communities.

Q: Ocean surface temperatures have risen 0.8°C over the past century due to climate change. Warmer water holds less dissolved oxygen and is more strongly stratified (thermoclines become sharper). Using what you know about phytoplankton, nutrients, and the food web, what is a likely effect on marine ecosystems?

Phytoplankton require nutrients (especially nitrogen and phosphorus) upwelled from depth. Stronger thermal stratification acts as a physical barrier: cold, nutrient-rich deep water can't mix upward as easily into the warm photic zone. Result: nutrient-limited phytoplankton in the surface layer. Satellite data already shows a 6–10% decline in ocean chlorophyll concentration in some tropical regions over the past 25 years — consistent with this mechanism. A smaller phytoplankton base cascades upward through every trophic level — this is a bottom-up effect triggered by physical oceanography changes. This is an active area of climate science research.

Quick Summary

  • The ocean is 71% of Earth's surface and 99% of Earth's habitable volume by space
  • Five depth zones: epipelagic (sunlit), mesopelagic, bathypelagic, abyssal, hadal — each with distinct communities
  • Phytoplankton produce ~50% of Earth's oxygen; open ocean is low-productivity but vast
  • Chemosynthesis: bacteria use chemical reactions (H₂S oxidation) as energy source instead of light — discovered at hydrothermal vents in 1977
  • Whale falls create temporary ecosystems lasting 50–100 years; sulphophilic stage mimics vent chemistry
  • Ocean systems use the same frameworks (energy flow, nutrient cycling, feedback) as terrestrial systems — but at vastly different scales, depths, and chemistries
