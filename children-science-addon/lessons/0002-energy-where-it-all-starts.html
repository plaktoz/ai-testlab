Energy: Where It All Starts

Lesson 02 · ~30 minutes

Every single bite of food you have ever eaten was sunlight.

Not a metaphor. The sandwich you had for lunch was built from wheat — which captured photons 3 months ago, dismantled CO₂ molecules from the air, and used the energy in those photons to glue carbon atoms into starch. Those starch molecules are now in your blood as glucose. You are running on light that hit a field somewhere in early spring.

If the sun switched off tonight, every food chain on Earth would collapse within weeks.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: explain how photosynthesis works from first principles (atoms, not labels), state and apply the 10% energy rule, use the rule to explain why food chains are short and top predators are always rare, and calculate the biomass pyramid for a given chain.

Photosynthesis: The First Principles Version

Almost every biology course tells you the equation. Almost none explain why it works. Start here:

Air is ~0.04% CO₂. A CO₂ molecule is one carbon bonded to two oxygens. The carbon is the interesting part — it's the scaffold for every organic molecule (sugars, fats, proteins, DNA). Plants need carbon atoms to build their bodies, but CO₂ is a low-energy molecule — you can't run a cell on it directly.

So plants do something radical: they use photons of light to break the bonds in water molecules (H₂O → H⁺ + O²⁻), releasing electrons with enough energy to power a chemical reaction that converts CO₂ into glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆). The oxygen is a waste product — which is why breathing is possible at all.

The equation:
6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose) + 6 O₂

Read this as: "Take carbon atoms from air, hydrogen atoms from water, use sunlight to glue them together into sugar, and throw away the oxygen." Every organism on Earth — including you — runs on the glucose or its derivatives.

Q: A plant grows from a tiny seed into a 50 kg tree. Where does most of that 50 kg of physical mass come from?

Most of a tree's dry mass is carbon — and that carbon came from CO₂ in the air via photosynthesis. Water provides the hydrogen atoms. Soil minerals are important but make up only a small fraction of total mass (calcium, potassium, etc.). Sunlight provides energy to drive the reaction, not matter. A tree is essentially solidified air.

Why Only 10% Makes It to the Next Level

When a rabbit eats grass, it doesn't convert 100% of the grass into rabbit. Not even close. Here's what actually happens to the energy:

  • ~60% is never absorbed — it passes out as faeces (undigested fibre)
  • ~30% is burned for the rabbit's own metabolism — keeping warm, moving, heart pumping
  • ~10% becomes actual rabbit biomass (muscle, fat, bone)

This is the 10% rule: roughly 10% of the energy at one trophic level makes it into biomass at the next. The other 90% is lost — mostly as heat (metabolic waste) and faeces.

This is not inefficiency. This is thermodynamics. The second law of thermodynamics says every energy conversion generates heat. Life doesn't get a special exemption.

Energy pyramid — same food chain, the numbers tell the story

Grass (producers) 10,000 kg
Rabbits (herbivores) 1,000 kg
Foxes (secondary consumers) 100 kg
Eagle (tertiary consumer) 10 kg
Apex ~1 kg

Each level has roughly 10% of the biomass of the level below it. This is why top predators — wolves, tigers, orcas — are always rare. The energy base doesn't support large numbers at the top. There aren't many wolves in Yellowstone not because wolves are being persecuted, but because physics doesn't allow 10,000 kg of wolves to exist if there are only 10,000 kg of elk.

Q: Using the 10% rule: if a grassland stores 100,000 kg of grass biomass, how much wolf biomass can it ultimately support at the top of a 4-level food chain (grass → deer → wolf)?

Grass: 100,000 kg → Deer: 10,000 kg (10%) → Wolf: 1,000 kg (10%). That's a 3-level chain (grass is level 1, deer level 2, wolf level 3). At 1,000 kg, that's roughly 10 adult wolves (each ~100 kg). In a real ecosystem this is exactly why wolf pack territories are enormous — they need hundreds of square kilometres of prey to sustain even a small pack.

Why Food Chains Are Short

Most food chains in nature have 3–5 levels, almost never more than 6. Now you can see why. Start with 1,000,000 kJ of solar energy captured by plants:

Plants: 1,000,000 kJ → Herbivores: 100,000 kJ → Carnivore 1: 10,000 kJ → Carnivore 2: 1,000 kJ → Carnivore 3: 100 kJ

By level 5, there's barely enough energy to sustain a single individual animal. A 6th level is mathematically possible but practically impossible — there isn't enough energy left to support a viable breeding population. The chain stops not because nothing would like to eat the top predator, but because the energy base can't support it.

Q: Why are top predators (like tigers or great white sharks) always rare compared to herbivores in the same ecosystem?

The 10% rule is the fundamental cause. A and B and C may also be true, but they're consequences of the same constraint, not independent causes. Because so little energy makes it to the top trophic level, the ecosystem can support only a small number of top predators. Those animals need large territories because prey density is lower than they need — which is again the 10% rule expressed spatially.

Producers Are Not All Equal

Different producers capture solar energy at different rates. This rate is called primary productivity.

  • Tropical rainforest: ~2,200 g C/m²/year — massive canopy, year-round sun and rain
  • Temperate forest: ~600 g C/m²/year — seasonal, less sun in winter
  • Open ocean: ~125 g C/m²/year — nutrient-poor, despite all that sun
  • Desert: ~90 g C/m²/year — sun in abundance, water the bottleneck
  • Coral reef: ~2,500 g C/m²/year — among the highest on Earth, despite clear water

The productivity of the producer layer sets a hard ceiling on everything above it. That's why coral reefs support extraordinary biodiversity (high productivity base) and open oceans look relatively empty (low productivity base).

Q: An open ocean and a coral reef are both in the same part of the Pacific. The ocean supports 20× less fish biomass per square km than the reef. What's the most fundamental reason?

It comes down to the energy base. Coral reefs have algae embedded in coral tissue plus seaweed and phytoplankton — enormous primary productivity in a small area. The open ocean is nutrient-poor despite abundant sunlight (no nutrient upwelling), so phytoplankton is sparse. Less primary productivity → less biomass at every trophic level above it. The 10% rule compounds: 20× more producers means ~20× more herbivores means ~20× more carnivores.

Quick Summary

  • Photosynthesis converts CO₂ + H₂O + light into glucose. The carbon comes from air.
  • 10% rule: ~10% of energy at one trophic level becomes biomass at the next
  • 90% is lost as heat and faeces — this is physics, not inefficiency
  • Food chains are short (3–5 levels) because energy runs out
  • Top predators are rare because the energy pyramid narrows sharply
  • Primary productivity caps everything above it
