The Nitrogen Cycle

Lesson 05 · ~30 minutes

78% of every breath you take is nitrogen gas (N₂). You can't use any of it.

Nitrogen is in the air in enormous abundance — it makes up most of the atmosphere. And yet, nitrogen deficiency is the most common reason why plants fail to grow in natural ecosystems and on farms. The entire global food supply depends on solving the problem of getting atmospheric nitrogen into a form life can use.

Two bacteria species — Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter — do most of the heavy lifting. Without them, there would be no fertile soil. No crops. No protein. No life as we know it. You've probably never heard of either of them.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: explain why N₂ is inaccessible to most organisms, describe all four steps of the nitrogen cycle with the specific bacteria involved, explain what legumes do that regular crops can't, and apply nitrogen cycle knowledge to explain why fertiliser runoff causes ecological damage.

Why N₂ Is Useless to Almost Everything

Nitrogen is essential for life — it's in every amino acid (protein building block), every nucleotide (DNA building block), and chlorophyll. You are about 3% nitrogen by mass.

But N₂ — the gas form — has an almost unbreakable triple bond (N≡N). Breaking that bond requires enormous energy. The Haber-Bosch industrial process that makes synthetic fertiliser does it with very high temperature (~450°C) and pressure (~200 atm), consuming ~1–2% of global energy supply. That's how hard it is.

Certain bacteria, however, have an enzyme called nitrogenase that can break this bond at room temperature. They're the only organisms on Earth with this ability. Everything else must wait for them.

Q: Why can't a plant growing in perfectly aerated, moist soil just absorb N₂ directly from the air spaces in the soil?

It's a chemistry problem: the N≡N triple bond is one of the strongest in nature. Without nitrogenase (which only certain bacteria possess), there's no way to convert N₂ into usable forms like NH₄⁺ (ammonium) or NO₃⁻ (nitrate). Soil air is actually ~78% N₂ — plenty of it. The constraint is entirely biochemical, not physical abundance.

Step 1: Nitrogen Fixation

The first step: converting atmospheric N₂ into ammonia (NH₃) or ammonium (NH₄⁺), which plants can absorb. Done by nitrogen-fixing bacteria. Two main contexts:

Free-living fixation

Azotobacter and Clostridium live freely in soil and fix nitrogen independently. Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) do the same in aquatic systems. They fix N₂ using nitrogenase, creating NH₃ that gets released into the soil when they die.

Symbiotic fixation

Far more powerful: Rhizobium bacteria live inside root nodules of legume plants (peas, beans, clover, soy, acacia). The plant supplies the bacteria with sugar (energy from photosynthesis). The bacteria supply the plant with fixed nitrogen. It's a formal metabolic partnership.

This is why legumes are planted before wheat in crop rotation — they leave nitrogen-rich soil. The wheat that follows takes advantage of the nitrogen the legumes' bacterial symbionts have fixed. Farmers have known this empirically for 2,000 years; the microbiology was only understood in the 1880s.

The nodule deal: Rhizobium enters root hair cells, the plant surrounds them with a specialised structure (root nodule), and they begin exchanging: plant gives sucrose, bacteria give NH₄⁺. The plant even controls the oxygen levels inside the nodule (using a special haemoglobin called leghemoglobin — this is why legume root nodules are slightly pink) because nitrogenase is destroyed by O₂.

Q: A farmer grows soybeans (a legume) in a field for one season, then ploughs them in and plants wheat. Why does the wheat grow better than in a field where only wheat was grown last year?

The symbiosis between soybeans and Rhizobium converts atmospheric N₂ (unusable) into ammonium NH₄⁺ (usable). When the soy plants are ploughed in, that nitrogen enters the soil. Wheat is a heavy nitrogen user — nitrogen is essential for making the protein in grain. A wheat-after-legume rotation can produce comparable yields to wheat-with-synthetic-fertiliser, with zero industrial nitrogen input. This is the oldest sustainable agriculture technique there is.

Step 2: Nitrification

NH₄⁺ (ammonium) is useful but limited in how far it moves through soil. Most plants prefer nitrate (NO₃⁻) as their nitrogen source. Converting ammonium to nitrate is called nitrification, and it happens in two distinct reactions performed by two specific bacterial species:

NH₄⁺ (ammonium) → NO₂⁻ (nitrite) — toxic → NO₃⁻ (nitrate)
  • Nitrosomonas: converts NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ (nitrite). Nitrite is actually toxic to most organisms at high concentrations — this step is fast.
  • Nitrobacter: converts NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (nitrate). Nitrate is stable, soluble, and easily absorbed by plant roots.

These bacteria are autotrophic — they get their energy not from sunlight or eating other organisms, but from oxidising nitrogen compounds. They're chemolithotrophs: living on chemistry. You'll see the same strategy again at hydrothermal vents in Lesson 09.

Memory hook: Nitro-somonas goes first (NH₄ → NO₂), Nitro-bacter comes after (NO₂ → NO₃). "SO" before "BAC" — like "so, back to normal." This exact sequence is also the core of the aquarium nitrogen cycle in Lesson 07.

Q: A gardener adds ammonium-based fertiliser to a bed. Three weeks later, a soil test shows mostly nitrate (NO₃⁻), not ammonium. What happened?

This is exactly what nitrifying bacteria do. Within weeks of ammonium being added to well-aerated, moist soil (conditions that favour bacterial activity), Nitrosomonas converts it to nitrite and Nitrobacter finishes the job to nitrate. The gardener added NH₄⁺; the bacteria converted it. This is why many farmers prefer ammonium fertilisers — they're slower-releasing than straight nitrate (bacteria need time), reducing the chance of nitrate washing away in rain before plants can absorb it.

Step 3: Assimilation

Plants absorb NO₃⁻ through root hairs (using active transport — energy cost). Inside the plant, nitrate is converted back to ammonium and then incorporated into amino acids (primarily via an enzyme called glutamine synthetase). From there it's built into proteins, chlorophyll, and DNA. When an animal eats the plant, it assimilates that nitrogen into its own proteins. Nitrogen moves up the food web. This step is called assimilation.

Step 4: Denitrification — Returning N₂ to Air

What goes around comes around. Denitrifying bacteria (including Pseudomonas and Paracoccus) run the cycle in reverse: they convert NO₃⁻ → NO₂⁻ → N₂O → N₂, releasing nitrogen gas back to the atmosphere. They operate in anaerobic (oxygen-free) conditions — waterlogged soils, sediments, sewage systems.

Denitrification is what keeps atmospheric nitrogen from accumulating in soils indefinitely. Without it, all the nitrogen that fixation adds would build up in soil and water until concentrations became toxic. Denitrification is the "exhaust valve" of the nitrogen cycle.

N₂ (atmosphere) ↓ fixation NH₄⁺ (soil) ↓ nitrification NO₃⁻ (soil) ↓ plant absorption Plant/Animal protein ↓ death/excretion Decomposer → NH₄⁺ ↓ denitrification N₂ (atmosphere)

Q: A waterlogged field has very low oxygen levels in the soil. A farmer adds nitrate fertiliser. After a week, most of the nitrate is gone, but the plants show no improvement. What most likely happened?

Waterlogged soil = anaerobic conditions = ideal environment for denitrifying bacteria. They convert NO₃⁻ → N₂ and release it as gas. The fertiliser is essentially vented to the atmosphere. This is a real agricultural problem — nitrogen can be lost before plants can take it up. Solutions include improving drainage (reduce anaerobic zones) or using slow-release fertilisers that trickle into the soil when oxygen is present. Answer D (runoff) is possible but the question specifies the nitrate disappeared from the soil entirely, which points to denitrification.

When It Goes Wrong: Eutrophication Preview

What happens when humans add too much fixed nitrogen to a system? Fertiliser runoff carries excess NO₃⁻ into rivers and lakes. Phytoplankton and algae bloom explosively (they've been nitrogen-limited — now they have abundance). The bloom shades out underwater plants. When the bloom dies, decomposers consume it using enormous amounts of oxygen. Oxygen levels crash. Fish suffocate. A "dead zone" forms.

This process is eutrophication. You'll study it in detail in Lesson 06. But notice: it's the nitrogen cycle being overwhelmed by human addition — the same cycle that sustains all life when in balance.

Q: Final check — order these four nitrogen cycle steps correctly: nitrification, fixation, denitrification, assimilation

The sequence: (1) Fixation: N₂ → NH₄⁺ (bacteria convert unusable gas to usable ammonium). (2) Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (Nitrosomonas + Nitrobacter). (3) Assimilation: plants and animals incorporate NO₃⁻ into organic molecules. (4) Denitrification: after death and decomposition return nitrogen to NO₃⁻, denitrifiers convert it back to N₂. The cycle closes.

Quick Summary

  • N₂ is 78% of air but useless to plants/animals — the N≡N bond can't be broken without nitrogenase
  • Fixation: N₂ → NH₄⁺. Done by bacteria (Rhizobium in legume nodules, free-living Azotobacter)
  • Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ (Nitrosomonas) → NO₃⁻ (Nitrobacter)
  • Assimilation: plants absorb NO₃⁻ and build it into proteins; animals eat plants
  • Denitrification: anaerobic bacteria return NO₃⁻ → N₂ to atmosphere
  • Legumes + Rhizobium = natural fertiliser factory; basis of crop rotation for 2,000 years
  • Too much nitrogen (fertiliser runoff) → eutrophication → dead zones
