What Is a System?

Lesson 01 · ~30 minutes

In 1995, wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park after a 70-year absence. Within a few years, the riverbanks changed shape. Not because of the wolves eating fish — but because of a chain of events nobody predicted. Elk stopped grazing near river edges (because wolves patrol there). Vegetation grew back. Tree roots held the riverbanks together. Rivers narrowed and deepened.

Wolves changed rivers. That's what a system does.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: define an ecosystem from first principles (not from memory), explain the difference between biotic and abiotic components, describe a feedback loop in nature, and explain why removing any single piece of an ecosystem has consequences.

What Is a System?

Before "ecosystem", understand "system". A system has three things:

  1. Components — the parts (players, pieces, ingredients)
  2. Connections — how the parts interact (who eats who, what depends on what)
  3. Feedback — how changes ripple back and affect the system itself

A thermostat is a system: thermometer reads temperature (component), compares it to target (connection), turns heating on or off (feedback). A clock is not a system in this sense — it has no feedback; it just runs forward regardless of anything.

Ecosystems are systems. They have components (living and non-living), connections (energy flow, predation, decomposition), and feedback (more prey → more predators → fewer prey → fewer predators → more prey). The Yellowstone wolf story is a feedback loop at landscape scale.

Q: What makes an ecosystem a "system" rather than just a list of species?

A system is defined by connections and feedback, not by size or complexity. A jar with soil, a plant, and a worm is already a system — changing the moisture changes the worm's activity which changes decomposition which changes plant growth. The components interact.

Biotic vs Abiotic: Two Types of Components

Ecosystem components split into two groups:

  • Biotic — all living things: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, archaea, protists. Everything made of cells that grows, responds, reproduces.
  • Abiotic — all non-living things: sunlight, temperature, rainfall, soil minerals, water chemistry (pH, salinity, dissolved oxygen), wind, rock type, altitude.

This distinction matters because the same biotic community (same species list) behaves completely differently under different abiotic conditions. Put a rainforest plant community in the Sahara — same species, zero ecosystem. Abiotic conditions set the rules for what's possible.

The key insight: Biotic components adapt to abiotic conditions, but also change them. Trees shade the ground, lowering soil temperature. Earthworms aerate soil, changing water drainage. Phytoplankton add oxygen to water. Living things modify their own abiotic environment — and then the modified environment feeds back on them.

Q: A forest fire burns through a pine forest, removing all the trees. Which of these is an abiotic change that immediately follows?

Deer moving and fungi decomposing are biotic responses. Seeds germinating is also biotic. Soil temperature rising is an abiotic change — the physical environment shifts immediately when the shade canopy is removed. This higher temperature will then drive which species can colonize next (a biotic consequence of an abiotic shift).

Inputs, Outputs, and Feedback

Think of an ecosystem like a machine with inputs and outputs:

  • Inputs: solar energy, rainfall, nutrients from weathering rock, CO₂ from air
  • Outputs: heat (from metabolism), water vapour (evapotranspiration), O₂ (from photosynthesis), sediment runoff
  • Internal cycling: carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus get used, released, reused — they don't leave the system, they circulate

Energy flows through (enters as sunlight, leaves as heat — one way). Matter cycles within (atoms are borrowed and returned, over and over). This is one of the most important distinctions in all of ecology.

Memory trick: Energy is like money you spend — it's gone once used. Matter is like renting a costume — it goes back when you're done and someone else wears it next.

A Forest as a System — Inventory

Let's catalogue a temperate forest using our framework. This is Białowieża Forest, Poland — one of the last primeval forests in Europe.

Białowieża — System Inventory
Component type Examples Role in system
Abiotic — energy Sunlight Powers everything
Abiotic — matter Soil, water, CO₂, minerals Raw materials for growth
Biotic — producers Oak, hornbeam, spruce Convert solar energy to biomass
Biotic — consumers European bison, lynx, woodpecker Transfer energy up food web
Biotic — decomposers Fungi (Armillaria), bacteria Return nutrients to soil

Q: If you removed the soil from the Białowieża forest — just the mineral soil — what would collapse first?

Soil is the foundation: it anchors roots, stores water, holds the mineral nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium) that plants absorb. Without soil, producers die. Without producers, every herbivore, carnivore, and decomposer loses its energy source. The collapse runs upward from the abiotic base. Remove an abiotic component and you kill everything that depends on it.

Feedback Loops: The Engine of Stability

Ecosystems don't just react once — they loop. Two types:

  • Negative feedback (stabilising): a change triggers a response that reduces the change. Predators increase → prey decrease → predators starve → predators decrease → prey recover. This keeps population sizes bouncing around a stable average. Most natural cycles are driven by negative feedback.
  • Positive feedback (amplifying): a change triggers more of the same change. Warming → permafrost melts → releases methane → more warming → more permafrost melts. Positive feedback runaway. Rare in stable ecosystems, catastrophic when it occurs.

Q: A pond's algae population explodes after a warm summer. Fish eat algae and multiply, then algae drops back to normal. What type of feedback is this?

Negative feedback — the initial change (algae growth) triggered a response (fish growth) that reversed the change (algae dropped back). The system is self-correcting. This is how most ecosystems maintain their structure over time. Without this, any small perturbation would grow until the system collapsed.

Why This Matters for Everything Else

Every other lesson builds on this framework. When you see a food web, you're looking at connections. When you see the nitrogen cycle, you're looking at how matter circulates internally. When you see a trophic cascade (wolves → riverbanks), you're seeing feedback operating across an entire landscape.

The question to ask about any ecosystem is always the same: What are the components, what connects them, and what happens when something changes?

The Yellowstone explanation: Wolves (biotic addition) → elk avoid rivers (behaviour change) → riverbank vegetation grows (abiotic/biotic interaction) → roots stabilise banks → rivers narrow/deepen (abiotic change) → more fish habitat. Wolves changed rivers through a chain of connections, not through direct action. That's a system.

Quick Summary

  • A system = components + connections + feedback
  • Ecosystem components are biotic (living) or abiotic (non-living)
  • Energy flows through (sun in, heat out). Matter cycles within.
  • Negative feedback = stabilising. Positive feedback = runaway.
  • Removing any component — biotic or abiotic — has downstream consequences
