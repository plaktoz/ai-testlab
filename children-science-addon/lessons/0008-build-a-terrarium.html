Build a Terrarium

Lesson 08 · ~30 minutes · Hands-on

In 1960, David Latimer planted a single spiderwort cutting in a glass bottle, watered it once, sealed it shut — and didn't open it again for 53 years.

When he finally did open it in 2013, the plant was still alive. Perfectly healthy. The bottle contained a complete, closed ecosystem: the plant photosynthesised, produced oxygen, and created organic matter. Bacteria decomposed dead leaves, releasing CO₂ back to the plant. Water evaporated up the walls, condensed, dripped back to the soil. All four cycles — water, carbon, nitrogen, energy — ran continuously in a sealed glass bottle for over five decades.

This lesson is about building something like that. And understanding, from first principles, exactly why each layer you add is there.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: explain what cycles operate inside a closed terrarium, justify each physical layer of a terrarium build, identify which plants and conditions suit a closed vs open terrarium, and design a functional terrarium using knowledge from Lessons 01–07.

The Terrarium as an Ecosystem

A terrarium is not decoration. It's an engineered ecosystem. Let's map it against Lesson 01's framework before building anything:

  • Inputs: Light (photosynthesis energy), CO₂ (from air, before sealing), initial water, mineral nutrients in the soil
  • Outputs from a closed terrarium: None — that's the point. If truly sealed, matter cycles internally indefinitely. Only energy enters (as light) and leaves (as heat).
  • Internal cycling:
    • Water cycle: plant transpiration + surface evaporation → water vapour → condensation on glass walls → drips back to substrate
    • Carbon cycle: plant photosynthesis (CO₂ → organic carbon) → decomposition → CO₂ returned
    • Nitrogen cycle: bacteria in soil decompose dead plant matter → NH₄⁺ → nitrification → NO₃⁻ → plant assimilation → organic nitrogen in tissue → death → back to bacteria
    • Energy flow: light → plant (via photosynthesis) → decomposers (when plant dies) → heat (lost). Unlike matter, energy doesn't cycle — it flows in and out as heat.
  • Feedback: If CO₂ rises (too much decomposition), plants photosynthesise faster, using it up. If CO₂ falls (too much plant growth), photosynthesis slows. This is the same negative feedback loop that operates in every forest on Earth.

Q: A closed terrarium is placed in complete darkness for 2 weeks. The plants die. Then the dead leaves are left to decompose for a month. What happens to the CO₂ level inside the terrarium during the decomposition month?

Decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down organic carbon in dead tissue via aerobic respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy. The carbon that was locked in the plant leaves is released as CO₂ back to the enclosed air. In a functioning (light-receiving) terrarium, that CO₂ would immediately be used by living plants. In the dark, plants are dead, so CO₂ just accumulates. Without a photosynthesiser to reuse it, the carbon cycle is open at the plant end but closed at the decomposer end — an imbalance that would eventually exhaust the O₂ supply too.

Closed vs Open Terrarium

Before building, you need to choose which type suits your goals and plant selection.

Closed vs Open Terrarium — Key Differences

Property Closed Open
Humidity 80–100% (tropical) Ambient (30–60%)
Best plants Mosses, ferns, peperomia, fittonias, nerve plants Cacti, succulents, echeveria, haworthia, aloe
Watering Once, then never (or rarely) Every 1–2 weeks
Maintenance Very low once established Regular but simple
Ecosystem function All cycles truly closed Partially open (water input, heat loss)
Failure mode Too much water (rot), direct sun (overheating) Underwatering, dry soil compaction

Recommendation for this lesson: Build a closed terrarium with tropical plants — the biology is richer, the cycling is more visible, and you can demonstrate the water cycle simply by watching condensation on the glass walls.

Q: You want to build a self-sustaining closed terrarium. Which plant combination would work best?

A closed terrarium maintains near-100% humidity internally. Cacti and succulents evolved for arid conditions — constant high humidity causes root rot within weeks. Sunflowers and tomatoes need direct sun and become too large. Oak seedlings grow fast and would crack the glass. Mosses, ferns, and tropical low-light plants (fittonias, peperomia) evolved for humid forest floors — exactly the conditions a sealed terrarium provides. Match the plant's native habitat to the conditions you're creating.

Build: Closed Tropical Terrarium

This is an engineering design problem. Every layer has a function justifiable from first principles.

Materials List — Closed Tropical Terrarium

· Glass container with airtight lid (fish tank, large jar, or purpose-built terrarium)
  — volume: at least 10L for stability; 20–40L ideal
· Drainage layer: small pebbles or aquarium gravel (2–3 cm)
· Separation layer: fine mesh or horticultural grit (prevents soil from mixing into drainage)
· Activated charcoal: thin layer on top of drainage (1 cm)
· Substrate: tropical terrarium soil or mix of coco coir + perlite + organic compost (5–8 cm)
· Plants: 1–3 species suited to high humidity and low-medium indirect light
  (moss, fittonia, mini-peperomia, baby ferns, selaginella)
· Decorative elements (optional): small stones, driftwood, miniature figurines
· Spray bottle of dechlorinated water
· Long tweezers or bamboo skewer for planting

Layer 1: Drainage (Pebbles, 2–3 cm)

Why it's there: Plant roots need oxygen. Waterlogged roots suffocate and rot. The drainage layer sits below the soil and receives excess water that percolates through. Gravity keeps this water below the soil, preventing the root zone from becoming anaerobic. Without it, a closed terrarium's water cycle would oversaturate the substrate and kill the plants via root rot within weeks.

This is the same principle as the hyporheic zone (the gravel layer beneath riverbeds) — a hidden reservoir that maintains aerobic conditions and supports huge microbial communities.

Layer 2: Activated Charcoal (1 cm)

Why it's there: A closed ecosystem accumulates metabolic waste products — tannins, organic acids, gases from anaerobic decomposition in deeper substrate. Activated charcoal (not barbecue charcoal — the surface-area-maximised horticultural kind) adsorbs these toxins. Think of it as the terrarium's kidney. In a small, closed system, toxic waste build-up can acidify the substrate and inhibit bacterial and plant function.

Layer 3: Substrate (Soil, 5–8 cm)

Why it's there: The soil is the nitrogen cycle engine. It contains bacteria (decomposers, nitrifiers, nitrogen fixers if using moss), plant roots, and stored nutrients. Deeper soil allows plant roots to anchor and grow. The specific mix matters:

  • Coco coir: retains moisture, good structure, resists compaction
  • Perlite: adds drainage, prevents soil compaction, keeps root zone aerated
  • Organic compost: initial nutrient load for plants + bacterial inoculum

Assembly Steps

Build Order

1. Clean the glass with diluted white vinegar (kills surface mould, rinse thoroughly)
2. Add drainage layer: 2–3 cm pebbles
3. Add mesh/grit separation layer
4. Add activated charcoal layer: 1 cm
5. Add substrate: 5–8 cm, gently pressed flat but not compacted
6. Create planting holes with tweezers or a skewer
7. Remove plants from pots — gently shake soil off roots
8. Plant with roots fully covered — do not bury stems
9. Add moss last (it fills gaps and creates humidity surface)
10. Lightly mist the interior walls and soil surface
11. Check: soil should be visibly moist but not waterlogged (squeeze a handful — no water drips)
12. Seal the container

First 2 weeks: Leave sealed. You will see condensation on the glass — this is the water cycle working. If condensation covers >50% of glass at all times, briefly open to release some moisture (too wet → root rot). If there's no condensation at all, add more water.

Q: You build a closed terrarium with the drainage layer, charcoal, and soil. Two weeks in, you notice the soil looks very wet, the glass is covered in condensation constantly, and a plant stem looks brown and slimy at the base. What happened and what can you fix?

Brown, slimy stem at the base = root rot = anaerobic conditions around roots = too much water. Condensation across the entire glass surface all day is a sign of oversaturation. A healthy terrarium has condensation on one side (usually the cooler side) but not complete coverage. Fix: open the lid partially for a few days to allow evaporative loss, then trim the rotting stem sections (dead tissue invites fungal mold that can spread). This is a closed-system feedback failing — too much input during setup unbalanced the cycle before bacterial communities could establish and regulate.

All Four Cycles in Your Terrarium

Trace the actual paths happening inside your sealed glass container:

Light enters glass → Plant photosynthesis: CO₂ + H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + O₂ → Plant tissue grows (carbon + nitrogen stored) → Old leaves die, fall to soil → Bacteria/fungi decompose: organic C → CO₂, organic N → NH₄⁺ → Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₃⁻ → Plant roots assimilate NO₃⁻ → protein synthesis

Simultaneously, the water cycle runs:

Soil moisture → Plant roots absorb water → up through stems → Transpiration: water vapour through leaves → Condensation on glass walls → Drips back to soil

Energy is the only thing that doesn't cycle. Light comes in, drives photosynthesis, and ultimately leaves as heat. Without a continuous light input, the terrarium's energy budget runs to zero and all metabolism eventually stops.

Quick Summary

  • A closed terrarium is a miniature ecosystem where matter cycles internally — only energy (light) enters and leaves as heat
  • All four cycles operate: water (transpiration → condensation), carbon (photosynthesis → decomposition), nitrogen (decay → nitrification → assimilation), energy (light → plant → decomposers → heat)
  • Each physical layer has a function: drainage (prevents root anaerobia), charcoal (toxin adsorption), soil (nitrogen cycle + plant support)
  • Closed terrariums need high-humidity tropical plants; open terrariums suit arid plants
  • The most common failures: overwatering (root rot) and direct sunlight (temperature spike kills bacteria and plants)
  • David Latimer's bottle garden ran for 53 years on a single watering — testimony to what a balanced ecosystem can do in a closed system
