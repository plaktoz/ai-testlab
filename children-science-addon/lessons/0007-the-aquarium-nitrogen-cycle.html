The Aquarium Nitrogen Cycle

Lesson 07 · ~30 minutes · Hands-on

Every year, millions of pet fish die within weeks of being brought home. The killer is invisible.

It's not disease. Not bad food. Not cold temperature. It's ammonia — the same chemical fish excrete through their gills — building up in a new tank before the bacteria that would normally neutralise it have had time to grow. This is called New Tank Syndrome, and it kills fish faster than almost any other mistake in the hobby.

The fix is not a chemical. It's biology: the nitrogen cycle. Understanding it is the difference between a thriving aquarium and a graveyard. And here's the real insight — you are not just a fish-keeper. You are a microbiologist maintaining a miniature ecosystem.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: trace the full aquarium nitrogen cycle (NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻), name the bacteria responsible at each stage, explain why new tanks are toxic, describe how to cycle a tank safely, and design a basic maintenance schedule for a 60L freshwater aquarium.

Why Fish Waste Is Dangerous

Fish produce ammonia (NH₃) constantly. Unlike humans who convert nitrogen waste to urea and excrete it via urine, fish excrete ammonia directly through their gills. They're essentially exhaling poison. In a natural river or lake, the volume of water is so large that ammonia dilutes to harmless levels. In a 60-litre tank, it accumulates.

Ammonia (NH₃) is toxic at very low concentrations. At pH above 7, it exists as ammonia (NH₃ — the dangerous form). At pH below 7, it converts to ammonium ion (NH₄⁺ — less toxic). Both are measured together as "total ammonia nitrogen" (TAN). A reading above 0.5 mg/L starts stressing fish. Above 2 mg/L, fish develop ammonia burns — their gills literally corrode.

Fish excrete nitrogen as: NH₃ (through gills, ~90%) + small amounts in urine. Uneaten food and dead plant material also decompose into ammonia via bacteria. Every input of organic nitrogen in a tank eventually becomes ammonia unless something converts it.

Q: A new 60L tank has been set up for 3 days with 6 small fish. The owner notices the fish are gasping at the surface. The temperature is fine, the filter is running. What is the most likely cause?

New Tank Syndrome. In a brand-new tank, there are no nitrifying bacteria. Fish produce ammonia from day one; with no bacteria to convert it, ammonia rises rapidly. Fish gasping at the surface seek the slightly better-oxygenated water right at the surface AND exhibit gill damage symptoms (ammonia burns make it harder to extract oxygen). Temperature and pH might be fine — the problem is pure chemistry. Testing the water would show ammonia levels well above safe limits.

The Three Stages: NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻

The aquarium nitrogen cycle is the same process described in Lesson 05, compressed into a small box. Three stages:

NH₃ (ammonia) → Nitrosomonas bacteria → NO₂⁻ (nitrite) → Nitrobacter bacteria → NO₃⁻ (nitrate) → Water changes or plants

Stage 1: Ammonia → Nitrite (Nitrosomonas)

Nitrosomonas bacteria oxidise ammonia to nitrite: NH₃ + 1½O₂ → NO₂⁻ + H⁺ + H₂O. They get their energy from this reaction (they're chemolithotrophs — Lesson 05 vocabulary). Nitrosomonas colonise the biological filter medium — typically porous ceramic rings, sponge, or lava rock — wherever there's high surface area, water flow, and oxygen.

Nitrite (NO₂⁻) is also toxic to fish. It interferes with haemoglobin's ability to carry oxygen — fish effectively suffocate even in oxygenated water. During cycling, nitrite spikes after ammonia spikes. The phase where both ammonia and nitrite are elevated is the most dangerous period.

Stage 2: Nitrite → Nitrate (Nitrobacter)

Nitrobacter bacteria oxidise nitrite to nitrate: NO₂⁻ + ½O₂ → NO₃⁻. Nitrate (NO₃⁻) is much less toxic — fish tolerate it up to ~40–50 mg/L in most freshwater species. It still accumulates over time, which is why regular water changes are necessary.

In planted tanks, aquatic plants absorb nitrate directly as a nitrogen source — the same assimilation step from Lesson 05. Dense planting can reduce how often you need water changes.

Q: A fish keeper tests their new tank every 2 days for 4 weeks. Week 1: ammonia high, nitrite zero, nitrate zero. Week 2: ammonia falls, nitrite rises sharply, nitrate zero. Week 3: ammonia zero, nitrite falls, nitrate rises. Week 4: all stable. What does this pattern tell them?

This is a textbook cycling profile. Nitrosomonas establish first because they have an immediate substrate (ammonia) from the moment fish are added. They consume ammonia and produce nitrite — so ammonia falls as nitrite rises. Nitrobacter then colonise, consuming the accumulated nitrite and producing nitrate — so nitrite falls as nitrate rises. Once both colonies are established, the cycle runs continuously and both ammonia and nitrite stay near zero. The tank is now "cycled" and safe for more fish.

The Biological Filter

The "filter" in aquarium-keeping does three things — and only one of them is what most beginners think it does:

  • Mechanical filtration: physical media (filter floss, fine sponge) traps solid particles — uneaten food, fish waste, plant debris. This improves clarity but does not remove ammonia.
  • Chemical filtration: activated carbon adsorbs dissolved organic compounds, medications, and some toxins. Not relevant to the nitrogen cycle; wears out.
  • Biological filtration: porous media (ceramic rings, bio-balls, sponge) provides vast surface area for Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter to colonise. This is the nitrogen cycle in action. This is what keeps fish alive.
Never clean your biological filter media with tap water. Tap water is chlorinated specifically to kill bacteria. Rinsing your bio-media in tap water kills your nitrifying colony, instantly uncycling your tank. Use tank water when cleaning filter media — same temperature, no chlorine. This is one of the most common and most devastating beginner mistakes.

Q: A well-established 60L tank has been running well for 6 months. The owner cleans the entire filter thoroughly — including all the ceramic bio-rings — in fresh tap water, then reassembles it. Three days later, fish start dying. Why?

Classic New Tank Syndrome in a mature tank — caused by destroying the bacterial colony. The ceramic rings look clean but they're now biologically dead. Ammonia starts climbing immediately because fish keep producing it but nothing converts it. Three days is about right for a toxic build-up in a stocked tank. The correct approach: rinse filter media only in used tank water squeezed out during water changes — never tap water. The mechanical parts (pump, impeller, spray bar) can be cleaned with tap water safely.

Cycling a New Aquarium: A Hands-On Plan

There are two main approaches to cycling a 60L tank. Both work. Your choice depends on patience and whether you want fish in the tank while cycling.

Method A: Fishless Cycling (Recommended)

Add ammonia without fish, let the bacteria colony build up before adding any animals. Safer, ethical, faster at establishing large colonies.

60L Fishless Cycling — Week by Week

Equipment needed:
· 60L glass tank with lid
· Hang-on-back or canister filter with ceramic bio-rings
· Heater (set to 26°C — warmer = faster bacterial growth)
· Liquid test kit: ammonia, nitrite, nitrate (strips are inaccurate — buy liquid)
· Pure ammonia (unscented, no surfactants — read the label carefully) OR a pinch of fish food daily
· Dechlorinator (sodium thiosulphate — removes chlorine from tap water)

Week 1:
· Fill tank with tap water + dechlorinator
· Set filter and heater running
· Add ammonia to reach 2–4 mg/L (pure ammonia: ~0.1–0.3 ml/L; varies by product — test!)
· Test daily: expect ammonia to stay high
· Keep ammonia between 2–4 mg/L by adding small amounts as needed

Week 2:
· Nitrite should start rising as Nitrosomonas colonise
· Ammonia drops are a good sign
· Nitrate still zero — Nitrobacter not yet established
· Continue dosing ammonia to 2 mg/L daily (Nitrosomonas need a constant substrate)

Week 3–4:
· Nitrite spikes (can reach 10–20 mg/L — alarmingly high)
· Nitrobacter begin colonising
· Nitrate starts appearing
· Keep adding ammonia — bacteria need food to grow

Cycle complete when:
· Add ammonia to 2 mg/L → retest after 24 hours → ammonia = 0, nitrite = 0, nitrate elevated
· The whole colony converts 2 mg/L ammonia in under 24 hours
· Do a 50% water change to lower nitrate below 20 mg/L
· Add fish gradually — never all at once (too much ammonia spike at once)

Typical timeline: 4–6 weeks at 26°C. Shorter with bacterial starter cultures.

Method B: Fish-In Cycling

Add a few hardy fish (zebra danios, white cloud mountain minnows) and perform large (30–50%) daily water changes to keep ammonia below 0.5 mg/L while the colony builds. More stressful for fish, slower due to water changes diluting bacteria. If you've already bought fish and can't return them, this is the responsible approach — just test daily and do water changes whenever ammonia or nitrite rises above 0.25 mg/L.

Bacterial starter cultures: Commercially available products (e.g. "Dr. Tim's One and Only", "API QuickStart") contain live Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter. They can reduce cycling time to 1–2 weeks. Quality varies by product and how the cultures were stored. Alternatively, add filter media from a healthy, established tank — you're transplanting the bacteria directly. This is the fastest method.

Q: It is 5 weeks into a fishless cycle. Ammonia tests at 2 mg/L at 8am. At 8pm the same day it reads 0 mg/L and nitrite reads 0 mg/L. Nitrate is at 35 mg/L. What should you do next?

This is the "cycle complete" signal: 2 mg/L ammonia processed to zero in under 12 hours (some sources say 24 hours — faster is better). Zero nitrite confirms Nitrobacter is keeping up with Nitrosomonas output. Elevated nitrate confirms the cycle endpoint product is accumulating as expected. The only action needed is a water change to bring nitrate down before adding fish — 35 mg/L is borderline acceptable but a fresh start is better. Then add fish slowly: overcrowding day one re-spikes ammonia beyond what the colony can handle.

The Aquarium as a Miniature Ecosystem

Step back. What have we described?

  • Inputs: Fish food (organic nitrogen + carbon), light, CO₂
  • Outputs: NH₃ (fish), CO₂ (fish + bacteria respiration), removed via water changes
  • Internal cycling: NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (nitrogen cycle), CO₂ + light → plant biomass → fish eats plant → CO₂ (carbon cycle)
  • Feedback: more fish → more NH₃ → more bacterial growth → more conversion capacity → stabilising (negative feedback)
  • Carrying capacity: A 60L tank's bacterial colony can only handle a certain ammonia load. Adding too many fish exceeds the bacteria's conversion rate — ammonia builds, fish die. The tank has a biological carrying capacity just like any ecosystem.

Aquarium keeping teaches the nitrogen cycle because it shows it in action, in your bedroom, in real time. Every water test is a scientific measurement. Every fish death is data. Every successful colony establishment is ecology working exactly as it should.

Quick Summary

  • Fish excrete ammonia (NH₃) directly through gills — toxic above 0.5 mg/L
  • Nitrosomonas convert NH₃ → NO₂⁻ (nitrite — also toxic)
  • Nitrobacter convert NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (nitrate — much safer, removed by water changes/plants)
  • New Tank Syndrome: fish added before bacterial colonies established → ammonia spikes → fish die
  • Biological filter media must never be rinsed in chlorinated tap water
  • Fishless cycling: add ammonia, build colony, wait 4–6 weeks, then add fish gradually
  • An aquarium is a miniature ecosystem with real inputs, outputs, cycling, and carrying capacity
