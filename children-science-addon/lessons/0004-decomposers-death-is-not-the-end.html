Decomposers: Death Is Not the End

Lesson 04 · ~30 minutes

If decomposers stopped working tonight, Earth would be unliveable within a few decades.

Every organism that dies — every fallen tree, every dead insect, every whale that sinks to the ocean floor — would remain intact. Permanently. The carbon locked in those bodies would never return to the atmosphere. CO₂ levels would drop. Plants would have less carbon to build with. Photosynthesis would slow. The oxygen supply would thin.

Simultaneously, soils would stop receiving recycled nutrients. Every nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium atom tied up in dead matter would become permanently unavailable. Plants would starve. Everything above them would starve too.

Decomposers are not the cleanup crew. They are the system's engine.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: name the three main types of decomposers and explain what each does, trace the carbon cycle from death back to life, explain why decomposition rate varies with environment, and describe what humus is and why it matters.

Who Are the Decomposers?

Three main categories, all doing different parts of the same job:

1. Bacteria

The smallest and most numerous. A gram of healthy forest soil contains roughly 1 billion bacterial cells. They secrete enzymes directly into dead matter, digest it externally, and absorb the products. Bacteria are especially important in anaerobic environments (no oxygen) — like waterlogged soil, swamp sediments, and the inside of a compost heap. Different bacteria specialise in different molecules: some break down cellulose, some proteins, some fats.

2. Fungi

The heavy-machinery specialists. Fungi grow networks of fine threads called hyphae that physically penetrate wood, leaf litter, and soil. They produce powerful enzymes — notably ligninase — that break down lignin, the tough structural polymer in wood that almost nothing else can digest. Without fungi, fallen logs would persist for centuries. Fungi are why forests don't turn into landfills.

A famous example: Armillaria ostoyae in Oregon occupies 9.7 km² of forest and is estimated to be 8,000 years old. It's one organism. Most of it is underground, silently decomposing dead tree matter.

3. Detritivores

Animals that eat dead organic matter directly: earthworms, millipedes, woodlice, dung beetles, certain fly larvae. They don't fully chemically break down material — they shred and fragment it, vastly increasing the surface area available for bacteria and fungi to attack. Think of detritivores as the food processor that runs before the digestion happens.

Q: A fallen oak tree sits in a forest for 2 years. After 50 years it has almost completely disappeared. Which decomposer type is most responsible for breaking down the woody lignin in the trunk?

Lignin is one of the toughest biological molecules ever evolved — it's what makes wood structurally rigid. Very few organisms produce ligninase. Fungi (particularly white-rot fungi) are the primary agents. Without fungi, dead wood would persist for thousands of years, locking up carbon and nutrients permanently. Bacteria and detritivores assist, but fungi do the hardest chemical job. This is why log decomposition rate is strongly correlated with fungal diversity in the local soil.

The Carbon Cycle

Carbon is the skeleton of all life — every cell, every sugar, every protein contains carbon. The carbon cycle describes how carbon atoms move between living organisms, the atmosphere, soil, and water. Decomposers are the key returning mechanism.

CO₂ in atmosphere → Plant photosynthesis → Organic carbon in tissues → Eaten by animals → Death / excretion → Decomposers → CO₂ released (respiration)

There are two exits from the "organic carbon in tissues" box:

  1. Respiration: organisms burn glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy), releasing carbon as CO₂ directly back to atmosphere. This happens in every living cell, including yours, constantly.
  2. Death → decomposition: dead tissue passes to decomposers, who also respire it, releasing CO₂. Same outcome, different path.

There's also a geological long-term path: dead organisms buried under sediment, never fully decomposed, compressed over millions of years → fossil fuels. That's carbon removed from the active cycle for geological time — until we burn it.

Q: Trace the journey of a carbon atom: it starts in CO₂ in the atmosphere. It ends up back in CO₂. Fill in the sequence from this list: [plant respiration / photosynthesis / animal eaten / decomposition / taken into leaf]

The correct sequence: CO₂ in atmosphere → captured by plant via photosynthesis (through the leaf stomata) → carbon is now in plant glucose/cellulose → animal eats plant → carbon is in animal tissue → animal dies → decomposers break down tissue via respiration → CO₂ released back to atmosphere. The cycle is complete. Every step is a real physical/chemical transformation, not a metaphor.

Why Decomposition Rate Varies

The same dead leaf decomposes at radically different speeds in different environments. Understanding why reveals the chemistry underneath.

  • Temperature: Bacterial and fungal enzyme reactions run faster at higher temperatures (up to a point). A leaf takes ~1 month to decompose in a tropical forest but ~5 years in the Arctic tundra. That's why Arctic permafrost stores enormous amounts of ancient organic carbon — the cold simply slows the enzymes.
  • Moisture: Decomposers need liquid water to function. Deserts and very dry environments preserve organic matter (Egyptian mummies, leather found in salt mines). This is also why we refrigerate food — cold + low humidity slows bacterial metabolism.
  • Oxygen: Aerobic decomposition (with O₂) is ~10× faster than anaerobic. Waterlogged soils and swamp sediments decompose very slowly under anaerobic conditions — hence peat bogs preserving bodies for thousands of years.
  • Material toughness: Fruit decomposes in days (simple sugars). Bark takes months (lignin-rich). Bone takes years. The harder the molecular bonds, the longer it takes.
The peat bog paradox: Sphagnum moss grows in cold, waterlogged, oxygen-poor conditions. When it dies, it barely decomposes — it just piles up, slowly compressed into peat. Over thousands of years, peat becomes a massive carbon store. The UK's peat bogs store more carbon than all the forests of France and Germany combined. Draining a peat bog for farmland unleashes that carbon as CO₂.

Q: A tropical rainforest leaf is placed in four different conditions. In which does it decompose fastest?

Decomposition needs all three: warmth (faster enzyme reactions), moisture (bacteria/fungi need liquid water to function), and oxygen (aerobic decomposition is much faster than anaerobic). Option D provides all three. Option C is dry — fungi and bacteria can't function. Option B is cold — enzyme rate is low. Option A fails on all three counts. This is exactly why compost heaps need turning (oxygen), watering (moisture), and ideally some insulation to retain heat.

Humus: Decomposition's Gift to Soil

Not all dead matter breaks down completely. Some partially-decomposed organic material — complex carbon compounds that resist further breakdown — accumulates in soil as humus. Humus is dark (it's why fertile topsoil is dark brown), and it's extraordinarily valuable:

  • Water retention: humus holds water like a sponge — crucial for plant roots between rainfall events
  • Nutrient storage: humus particles are negatively charged, so they hold positively-charged mineral ions (calcium, potassium, magnesium) rather than letting them wash away
  • Soil structure: humus binds mineral particles into clumps (aggregates), creating air pockets that roots need

Agricultural topsoil takes ~500 years to build 1 cm of humus. Industrial farming that constantly tills the soil (destroying fungal networks and exposing humus to rapid oxidation) can degrade centuries of soil in decades. This is not abstract — current estimates suggest at current rates, the world has ~50–60 "harvests" worth of topsoil left.

Q: Two fields grow the same crops. Field A has been managed with cover crops and minimal tillage for 30 years. Field B has been ploughed every year and had no organic matter returned to it. Which field likely has more humus, and why does that matter for crops?

Field A has more humus for two reasons: cover crops return organic matter to the soil (builds humus), and minimal tillage preserves fungal networks and doesn't expose humus to rapid oxidation. Field B loses organic matter with each plough (fungi destroyed, humus oxidised to CO₂). More humus means Field A holds more water between rains, retains more nutrients, and has better structure — so plants have bigger, more extensive root systems. Field B requires more fertiliser and irrigation to achieve the same yield. Eventually it loses productivity without constant inputs.

Quick Summary

  • Three types: bacteria (ubiquitous, anaerobic capable), fungi (break down lignin with ligninase), detritivores (shred and fragment, increasing surface area)
  • Carbon cycle: CO₂ → plant → animal → death → decomposers → CO₂. Decomposers close the loop.
  • Decomposition rate increases with: temperature, moisture, oxygen. Decreases in cold, dry, or anaerobic conditions.
  • Humus is partially-decomposed organic matter that holds water, stores nutrients, and structures soil
  • Without decomposers: nutrients locked up, CO₂ depleted, photosynthesis crashes, ecosystem collapses
