Resilience and Collapse

Lesson 10 · ~30 minutes · Final Lesson

On 5 August 2010, a river in British Columbia turned bright orange.

The Fraser River system's salmon run was already stressed. Then a rockslide at Big Bar blocked upstream passage. Tens of thousands of sockeye salmon — 2.5 million strong in the run — were trapped below a 4-metre waterfall they couldn't clear. Bears had no salmon to eat. Bald eagles dispersed. The forest, which depends on salmon carcasses for 80% of its nitrogen input in the riparian zone, went unfertilised. The rockslide was fixed. But the ecosystem effect cascaded for years after.

One rockslide. One waterfall. A forest nitrogen deficiency that lasted half a decade.

Ecosystems resist most shocks. But every system has a breaking point — and when it breaks, it often doesn't return to what it was before. Understanding how and why systems collapse is the final piece of this course.
By the end of this lesson you will be able to: define resilience and distinguish it from resistance, explain why biodiversity increases resilience, describe tipping points and give a real example, explain extinction debt, and synthesise ideas from all 10 lessons into a unified framework for understanding any ecosystem.

Two Ways to Handle a Punch: Resistance vs Resilience

First, a crucial distinction:

  • Resistance: how well a system avoids being damaged by a disturbance. A boulder that doesn't move when pushed. A forest where a flood kills very few trees.
  • Resilience: how quickly a system returns to its pre-disturbance state after being changed. A rubber ball that bounces back. A forest that grows back to its original composition within 20 years after a fire.

These two properties trade off. A forest of ancient, slow-growing trees is highly resistant (hard to disturb) but poorly resilient (if disturbed, recovery takes centuries). A fast-growing weed community on disturbed soil is not very resistant (easily disturbed again) but highly resilient (grows back in a season). Many human interventions that increase resistance paradoxically reduce resilience — and vice versa.

Q: Fire suppression policies in many forests prevented fires for 50 years, allowing dead wood and thick undergrowth to build up. Then a fire did occur. Why was the resulting fire worse — and what does this illustrate about resistance vs resilience?

This is exactly what happened in Yellowstone (1988 fires), California, and many Australian ecosystems. Small, frequent fires clear dead wood and maintain a mosaic of vegetation structures — they are part of these ecosystems' normal functioning. Suppressing them increases short-term resistance (no small fires) but removes the system's natural self-regulating mechanism. Accumulated fuel eventually produces a fire far beyond what the system can bounce back from quickly. Natural fire regimes build resilience by keeping fuel load low. This is now widely understood — prescribed burns are deliberately used to restore resilience.

Biodiversity as Insurance

You've heard this through Lessons 03 and 05: species redundancy means multiple species do the same job. Now extend that idea to the whole resilience framework.

Biodiversity provides insurance in exactly the economic sense: you pay a premium (maintaining many species that don't all contribute at once) to protect against catastrophic loss (a function disappears because all species performing it died at once).

A 2012 study by Cardinale et al. (meta-analysis of 192 experiments) found that:

  • Higher species richness → higher productivity (more biomass produced)
  • Higher species richness → greater stability over time (less boom-bust variance)
  • The effect of biodiversity was comparable in magnitude to climate or nutrient inputs
  • Losing half the species in a community reduced ecosystem function by ~20–30%

Species may appear redundant during normal conditions. But in a drought, a pathogen outbreak, a heatwave — the one species that is specifically drought-tolerant, or pathogen-resistant, or heat-adapted suddenly becomes the only species that performs the function. The others collapsed. Redundancy is not waste; it is the portfolio.

The diversity–stability relationship: Robert MacArthur (1955) proposed that more complex food webs are more stable because energy has more pathways — if one is blocked, others compensate. This is now empirically supported but with nuance: it's functional diversity (variety of ecological roles) that matters most, not species count alone.

Q: A grassland has 40 grass species under normal conditions. In a drought year, 35 of them die. The 5 survivors are drought-tolerant. The ecosystem maintains 60% of its normal grass coverage. A monoculture farm next door (one grass species) drops to zero coverage in the same drought. Which principle does this demonstrate?

This is the textbook definition of the insurance effect (also called the portfolio effect). During normal years, all 40 species coexist and the drought-tolerant 5 are "wasted" from a productivity standpoint. When drought hits, those 5 are the difference between 60% function and zero function. The monoculture has no portfolio — one event wipes out the entire investment. This exact pattern has been observed in real Cedar Creek grassland experiments (Tilman et al.) running since 1996. It's one of the strongest arguments for conservation from a purely self-interested human perspective: biodiversity is infrastructure.

Tipping Points: When the System Doesn't Come Back

Most disturbances push a system away from its normal state — and it bounces back. But some disturbances push the system past a tipping point, after which the system reorganises around a completely different stable state and may not return even when the disturbance is removed.

The technical term is a regime shift. The key feature: it's non-linear. A small additional stress can produce a disproportionately large and irreversible change.

Four real examples:

1. Lakes: Clear Water → Turbid Algae

We covered this in Lesson 06. A clear, macrophyte-dominated lake can receive decades of slowly increasing nutrient input and remain clear — the macrophytes absorb nutrients and shade out algae. Then, past a threshold, one hot summer tips it into an algae-dominated state. The algae now shade out the macrophytes. Even if nutrient input is reduced, the lake often stays turbid because the structural mechanism (macrophyte shading) that kept it clear is gone. Reversing it requires reducing nutrients far below the level that caused the flip.

2. Coral Reefs: Coral → Algal Turf

Healthy coral reefs maintain coral through grazing fish that eat algae. Remove the fish (overfishing) → algae overgrow coral. Coral bleaching (temperature) kills more coral → more algae. A reef can flip from 60% coral cover to <5% in a decade and not recover for generations even if fishing stops, because the structural complexity (3D reef architecture) that young coral needs to settle was lost when the adult coral died.

3. Amazon Rainforest: Forest → Savanna

The Amazon creates its own rainfall. Trees transpire ~20 billion tonnes of water vapour per day, generating "flying rivers" that provide 50–80% of the Amazon basin's rainfall. Deforestation reduces transpiration → less rainfall → trees die → more deforestation. Scientists estimate a tipping point at around 20–25% deforestation of the original Amazon. The Amazon is currently at ~17% deforestation. Past the tipping point, the eastern Amazon transitions to savanna regardless of whether further cutting stops.

4. Atlantic Ocean Circulation (AMOC)

The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation moves warm surface water north and cold deep water south. It warms northwest Europe and drives global climate patterns. Freshwater from melting Greenland ice is reducing its density-driven force. Models suggest a possible collapse — not a slowdown, a collapse — at continued warming levels. A collapse would cool northwest Europe by 5–15°C within decades. This is the highest-stakes regime shift currently being monitored.

Q: A lake has been receiving fertiliser runoff for 20 years. Scientists reduce the nutrient input to zero. But 5 years later, the lake is still algae-dominated and turbid. Why hasn't it recovered?

This is "hysteresis" — the system's response to nutrient reduction follows a different path than its response to nutrient increase. Phosphorus accumulated in the sediment over 20 years of loading; when external input stops, the sediment continues releasing it (internal loading). The algae use this legacy phosphorus. Additionally, the macrophyte seed bank may have been exhausted, or sediment conditions now favour algae. Reversing a regime shift often requires intervention far below the threshold that caused it — not just removing the cause, but actively restoring the alternative stable state (reintroducing macrophytes, treating sediments, introducing grazing fish). Simple nutrient reduction is rarely sufficient.

Extinction Debt

One of the most important and underappreciated concepts in conservation ecology:

Extinction debt is the future extinctions that are guaranteed to happen as a result of habitat changes that have already occurred, even if those habitats are never disturbed again.

How it works: Species populations require minimum viable population sizes to survive long-term. Fragment a habitat → isolated sub-populations → each is small → vulnerable to random events (bad winter, disease, inbreeding) → eventual local extinction even with no further human action.

Real example: When old-growth forest is logged in the UK and replaced with smaller patches, the forest birds in those patches don't all disappear immediately. Some persist for decades. But the patch is too small to maintain viable populations indefinitely — extinction is delayed, not avoided. A study of UK woodland birds (Thomas et al., 2004) found that past habitat loss had committed roughly 25% of woodland bird populations to extinction before any further deforestation occurred.

The debt is real but unpaid. It will be collected by biology, over time, regardless of future policy.

Why this matters for conservation policy: Current extinction rates underestimate the eventual total because extinction debt hasn't been collected yet. When ecologists say "we're in the sixth mass extinction," they're including the species that are currently alive but demographically committed to extinction — not just the ones already gone.

A Synthesis: What You Now Know

Ten lessons. One framework. Apply it to any ecosystem:

The Complete Framework

1. Systems (Lesson 01): Every ecosystem has inputs, outputs, stocks, and feedback loops. Start by identifying these.

2. Energy (Lesson 02): Energy flows one way — in at the base (usually sunlight), degrades at each trophic level. Follow the energy to understand what limits population size.

3. Food Webs (Lesson 03): Real ecosystems are webs, not chains. Redundancy absorbs shocks. Keystone species exert disproportionate influence. Trophic cascades propagate changes unpredictably.

4. Decomposers (Lesson 04): Matter cycles; energy doesn't. Decomposers close the carbon cycle. Without them, nutrients lock up and the system starves.

5. Nitrogen (Lesson 05): Nitrogen availability controls growth more than almost any other factor. Bacteria are the key transformers. Human interference (Haber-Bosch, fertiliser) has doubled the amount of reactive nitrogen on Earth.

6. Aquatic Systems (Lesson 06): Water adds physical stratification. The same cycles operate but under different physical constraints. Eutrophication shows what happens when nutrient input overwhelms the system's self-regulation.

7. Applied Nitrogen (Lesson 07): The nitrogen cycle in a tank is the same as in nature — faster, visible, fatal when mismanaged. Real-world application of biochemistry.

8. Closed Systems (Lesson 08): All cycles in a bottle. Every component of a terrarium has a first-principles justification. Matter cycles indefinitely; energy does not.

9. Ocean (Lesson 09): 71% of the planet. Chemosynthesis proves life requires energy, not sunlight. Whale falls as ecosystem succession. Scale matters: small pond principles apply to huge oceans.

10. Resilience (this lesson): Redundancy = resilience. Tipping points exist. Extinction debt is real. Human actions are currently exceeding multiple ecological tipping points simultaneously.

Q: A coral reef has 300 species of fish. Half of all species are removed by a combination of warming, bleaching, and overfishing over 10 years. The reef still looks structurally intact. A further warm event bleaches 40% of remaining coral. The reef collapses — nearly all remaining species leave or die within 2 years. Using all the concepts from this course, which combination best explains the collapse?

Option A synthesises four lessons: redundancy (Lesson 03), tipping points and resilience (Lesson 10), extinction debt (Lesson 10), and the role of structural complexity in recovery (Lessons 03, 09). The reef's resilience was eroded by the first wave of species loss — not dramatically (it "still looked intact") but sufficiently that it had no buffer for the bleaching event. This is the pattern seen in real reefs: the Great Barrier Reef shows exactly this trajectory. Temperature is the trigger; reduced redundancy is why it doesn't recover. Neither alone explains the collapse; both together do. That's the whole point of studying systems.

Q: Final synthesis. An ecologist is studying a temperate grassland. She wants to understand its resilience. Which measurement gives the most complete picture?

Option B is correct because resilience is multi-dimensional. Total biomass (A) can be high in a monoculture with no resilience. Species count (C) matters but functional diversity matters more — 50 species of grass doing the same thing provides less insurance than 10 species filling 10 different functional roles. Primary productivity (D) tells you current health, not resilience to future stress. Functional diversity + species richness + soil health covers: the insurance pool (functional diversity), the redundancy backup (species richness), and the decomposer/nutrient cycling engine (soil). This is everything Lessons 01–10 point toward: a healthy ecosystem is not just productive — it is structurally complex, functionally diverse, and capable of absorbing shocks and returning.

Quick Summary

  • Resistance: how well a system avoids disturbance; resilience: how well it recovers after disturbance. These trade off.
  • Biodiversity is insurance — redundancy keeps functions running when individual species fail
  • Tipping points: threshold past which a system shifts to a new stable state and may not return. Real examples: lakes, coral reefs, Amazon, ocean circulation
  • Extinction debt: future extinctions committed by past habitat loss, not yet collected by biology
  • Human modification is currently pushing multiple systems past tipping points simultaneously
  • The framework from these 10 lessons applies to any ecosystem: identify inputs/outputs/cycles/feedback, trace the energy, map the web, find the tipping points
Final thought: You started this course asking "what is a system?" You can now answer that question for a wolf in Yellowstone, a bacterium in a fish tank, a phytoplankton in the Pacific, a dead whale on the abyssal plain, and a moss in a sealed bottle. They're all running the same underlying chemistry — energy flowing one way, matter cycling endlessly, feedback loops keeping it stable until they don't. Understanding that isn't just biology. It's how the world actually works.
