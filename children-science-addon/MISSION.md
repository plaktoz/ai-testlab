# Mission

## Why

A 12–15-year-old is surrounded by nature every single day — a spider in the corner, fungus on bread, a crow stealing chips, a dead fish floating in a pond — and has zero framework for why any of it matters or how it connects. That's a gap worth closing.

By the end of this course, the learner can look at *any* environment — a forest, a fish tank, even a jar of dirt — and explain it from first principles: where the energy comes from, how atoms cycle through living and non-living matter, who eats who, why the numbers stay in balance, and what happens when one piece gets knocked out. And they can prove they understand it by building something real.

The goal isn't to pass an exam. The goal is to see the world differently.

## Success looks like

- Can explain the 10% energy rule and use it to predict why food chains are short and why top predators are always rare
- Can trace atoms (carbon, nitrogen) through a complete cycle — from air to soil to organism and back — without notes
- Can predict a trophic cascade: "wolves vanish → what exactly happens to the riverbanks, and why?"
- Can explain why a new aquarium kills fish, and how to cycle a tank from first principles (NH₃ → NO₂ → NO₃)
- Can design a self-sustaining closed terrarium: choosing components, balancing cycles, predicting what breaks it
- Can compare forest, pond, and ocean ecosystems on the same framework (energy source, producers, key decomposers, nutrient cycles)
- Can distinguish a food chain (a useful lie) from a food web (messy reality) and explain why webs survive shocks better
- Can define and use without prompting: ecosystem, trophic level, keystone species, decomposer, nitrification, carrying capacity

## Constraints

- Age 12–15: treat them as intelligent — abstraction is fine, hand-holding is not
- **30 minutes per lesson** — every lesson is completable in one sitting, no exceptions
- Hook first, explain second — open every lesson with something surprising, gross, or counter-intuitive
- **First principles over facts**: always explain *why* before *what* — the mechanism, not the label
- Real species, real numbers, real places — no generic "animal eats plant" filler
- **Quiz every lesson** — retrieval practice baked in; no section should run 3+ paragraphs without interaction
- Revisit core ideas (10% rule, nitrogen cycle) across multiple lessons in different contexts to build storage strength
- Hands-on where possible: terrarium and aquarium lessons produce something the learner can point to
- Funny is a feature — unexpected analogies and gross facts aid retention

## Out of scope

- Conservation policy and human impact (worth its own course later)
- Climate science and long-term ecosystem change
- Advanced population modeling (Lotka-Volterra, predator-prey math)
- Genetics, evolution, speciation

## Lesson Roadmap

10 lessons × 30 minutes. Core ideas recur across lessons to build storage strength — the nitrogen cycle and 10% rule appear in multiple different ecosystems so the learner stops thinking of them as "things to memorise" and starts using them as lenses.

| #  | Title | Core First Principle | Hands-on / Quiz Focus |
|----|-------|---------------------|----------------------|
| 01 | What Is a System? | An ecosystem is an input/output/feedback machine. Biotic + abiotic together. | Quiz: identify every component in a forest scene — what's missing if you remove the soil? |
| 02 | Energy: Where It All Starts | Photosynthesis from atoms up. Why the 10% rule makes top predators rare and food chains short. | Quiz: calculate how many kg of grass it takes to grow 1 kg of wolf |
| 03 | Food Webs: The Messy Reality | Food chains are textbook fiction. Webs are real. Redundancy = stability. Keystone species. | Quiz: remove one species from a web and trace every downstream effect |
| 04 | Decomposers: Death Is Not the End | Fungi, bacteria, detritivores. The carbon cycle. Without decomposers the world drowns in unrotted corpses. | Quiz: trace a single carbon atom from a dead oak tree to the atmosphere and back into a living thing |
| 05 | The Nitrogen Cycle | N₂ is useless to almost all life. Soil bacteria fix it, plants use it, animals eat plants, decomposers return it. This cycle runs everything. | Quiz: label the full cycle; explain why legumes are planted before wheat |
| 06 | Aquatic Ecosystems: The Pond | Light zones, dissolved oxygen, phytoplankton as invisible forests. Eutrophication: when fertiliser kills a lake. | Quiz: diagnose a dying pond from a set of clues — reason from first principles |
| 07 | The Aquarium Nitrogen Cycle | Fish excrete NH₃ → Nitrosomonas bacteria → NO₂ → Nitrobacter bacteria → NO₃ → plants. Why brand-new tanks kill fish. How to cycle a tank. | Hands-on: write a step-by-step plan to cycle a new 60L aquarium safely |
| 08 | Build a Terrarium | Apply all four cycles at once: water, carbon, nitrogen, energy. Choose species. Predict what goes wrong. | Hands-on: design a closed-loop moss terrarium — justify every component from first principles |
| 09 | Ocean Ecosystems | 71% of Earth's surface. Photic zone. Chemosynthesis at hydrothermal vents (life without the sun!). Whale falls as temporary deep-sea ecosystems. | Quiz: compare ocean vs forest on the same framework used in Lesson 01 |
| 10 | Resilience and Collapse | Biodiversity is redundancy. What makes systems absorb shocks vs collapse? Extinction debt. Synthesise everything. | Quiz: given an ecosystem under stress, predict whether it survives — justify with lessons 1–9 |
